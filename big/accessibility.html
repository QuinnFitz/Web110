Accesibility


Accessibility is one of the primary factors to any great website. How easy is it for users to comprehend and navigate around your website? You can’t expect people to stay on a website if they can’t figure out how to find the information they’re looking for. If Wikipedia didn’t have a search bar, it’d be a far less functional website, that would have a lot less users.

Keep in mind also, that disabled users may have difficulty using youl, if you don’t make it more accommodating. For instance, having Alt text and strong page structures for screen readers. Not only will this help your accessibility, but it’ll actually boost your SEO, allowing even more people to find your website.

Making your website accessible will also make for a more appealing experience on your website, which will encourage people to return. It doesn’t matter how many users visit your site if they never come back.

Some accessibility standards include things like using headings to organize content, including alt text in images, and having unique, descriptive text for your links. When naming links, it’s important to assign the links to descriptive text. This helps not only visually-capable people, but also any visually-impaired users. Screen readers can scan a page for links, but will often not give the context of text prior to the link. So, it’s important to have descriptive text for your links so that they still make sense out of context.

Also, be careful when using certain color combinations on your webpage, such as red-green color combos, which about 8% of the population has trouble seeing. You also have to be careful to structure a page in a way that makes sense. One mistake a lot of web designers make, is to use tables for structure, and not for data. This is challenging for screen reader users, as the screen reader will tell the user how many columns and rows exist in the table. It may also read off the content in an order that was not intended, and that differs from the visual layout.
